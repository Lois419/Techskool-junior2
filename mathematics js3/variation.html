Variation

INTRODUCTION

Variation refers to the relationships between two or more quantities where a change in one quantity results in a change in the other(s).
The main types of variation are direct, inverse, joint, Partial.

Direct Variation:

  • When two quantities increase or decrease together at the same rate, they are in direct variation.  
  • The relationship can be expressed as y = kx, where y and x are the variables, and k is the constant of variation.
  • For example, if the cost of apples varies directly with the number of apples, then buying more apples means a higher total cost.

    • Indirect Variation:

  • When one quantity increases as the other decreases, they are in inverse variation.  
  • The relationship can be expressed as y = k/x, where y and x are the variables, and k is the constant of variation.
  • For example, if the speed of a vehicle increases, the time it takes to travel a certain distance decreases, assuming distance is constant.

    • Joint Variation:

  • Joint variation occurs when a quantity varies directly with the product of two or more other quantities  
  • For example, if z varies jointly with x and y, the relationship can be expressed as z = kxy, where k is the constant of variation.

    • Partial Variation:

  • Partial variation is a combination of direct and constant variation.  
  • It means a quantity varies directly with another quantity and also has a fixed value (constant).
  • For example, if y partly varies with x, the relationship can be expressed as y = kx + c, where k is the constant of variation and c is the constant value.
